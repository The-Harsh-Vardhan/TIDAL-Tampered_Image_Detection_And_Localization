# Doc 7.5 — Visualization, Evaluation, and Failure Analysis

## 1. Introduction

Quantitative metrics such as Intersection over Union (IoU), Pixel-F1, and AUC-ROC provide aggregate summaries of model performance, but they obscure the spatial structure of predictions. In tampered image detection and localization, where the output is a dense pixel-level mask delineating manipulated regions, numeric scores alone are insufficient to assess whether a model is forensically useful. A model that achieves a moderate F1 score may still produce spatially coherent masks that closely follow tampering boundaries, or it may produce diffuse, noisy predictions that happen to overlap the ground truth by chance. Only visual inspection can distinguish these two outcomes.

Qualitative visualization serves three critical roles in this domain. First, it enables direct verification of localization accuracy — confirming that predicted tampered regions correspond to actual manipulations rather than to dataset artifacts such as compression boundaries or homogeneous backgrounds. Second, it exposes systematic failure modes that are invisible in aggregate statistics: over-segmentation in textured regions, consistent boundary erosion, or complete misses on specific forgery types. Third, it provides interpretable evidence for stakeholders who must assess whether a detection system meets forensic reliability standards.

This document describes the visualization and failure analysis methodology applied to a U-Net segmentation model with a ResNet34 encoder, trained for pixel-level tampering localization on the CASIA splicing and copy-move dataset.

---

## 2. Detection Visualization

The primary evaluation visualization is a four-panel layout that presents, for each test image, the following views side by side:

1. **Original Image** — the input RGB image as received by the model.
2. **Ground Truth Mask** — the binary annotation indicating tampered pixels, rendered in grayscale where white denotes manipulation.
3. **Predicted Tamper Mask** — the binarized model output after thresholding, displayed in the same grayscale convention.
4. **Overlay Visualization** — the original image with predicted tampered regions highlighted, typically as a semi-transparent red layer blended at 40% opacity over the underlying pixels.

This layout enables rapid visual assessment of three properties: spatial alignment between prediction and ground truth, the presence of false positive regions outside the tampered area, and false negative gaps within it. The overlay panel is particularly informative because it anchors the prediction to recognizable image content, making it straightforward to judge whether detected regions correspond to semantically meaningful manipulations or to irrelevant image features.

Overlay generation follows a direct compositing approach. For each pixel where the predicted mask is positive, the original RGB value is blended with a constant red channel: $\text{overlay}[i] = 0.6 \cdot \text{image}[i] + 0.4 \cdot [1, 0, 0]$. Pixels outside the predicted region remain unmodified. This produces a visualization that preserves image detail while clearly delineating the predicted tampering extent.

## 3. Probability Heatmaps

Binary masks discard information about model confidence. A pixel classified as tampered at a threshold of 0.5 may have a predicted probability of 0.51 or 0.99 — a distinction that the binary mask collapses. Probability heatmaps restore this information by displaying the raw sigmoid output as a continuous-valued spatial map prior to thresholding.

These heatmaps are rendered using perceptually uniform colormaps such as *magma* or *inferno*, where dark regions correspond to low tampering probability and bright regions correspond to high confidence. This visualization reveals several important phenomena. First, it identifies regions of weak but nonzero tampering signal — areas where the model detects some anomaly but with insufficient confidence to exceed the threshold. Second, it exposes uncertain boundary zones where probabilities transition gradually rather than sharply, indicating that the model struggles to resolve the precise edge of a manipulation. Third, it distinguishes between errors caused by poor feature extraction (low probability throughout the tampered region) and errors caused by suboptimal thresholding (moderate probability that falls just below the decision boundary).

When the optimal classification threshold is far from 0.5 — as is common in class-imbalanced settings where tampered pixels are a small fraction of the image — heatmap inspection becomes essential for diagnosing whether the threshold sweep is recovering genuinely detected regions or merely capturing noise.

## 4. Error Map Analysis

Error maps provide a pixel-level decomposition of model predictions into three categories, each rendered in a distinct color:

- **Green (True Positive):** Pixels that are tampered in the ground truth and correctly predicted as tampered. These represent successful detections.
- **Red (False Positive):** Pixels that are authentic in the ground truth but incorrectly predicted as tampered. These indicate over-segmentation or hallucinated detections.
- **Blue (False Negative):** Pixels that are tampered in the ground truth but missed by the model. These represent localization failures.

The spatial distribution of these categories reveals systematic model weaknesses with far greater specificity than scalar precision and recall values. Concentrated red regions adjacent to true positives suggest boundary over-expansion — the model detects the correct area but predicts a mask that extends beyond the actual manipulation. Scattered red patches in untampered areas indicate false alarms triggered by textures, edges, or compression artifacts that resemble tampering signals. Blue regions concentrated at the periphery of tampered areas point to boundary under-segmentation, while large contiguous blue zones indicate that the model has entirely missed a manipulation.

Error maps are generated by computing element-wise logical operations between the predicted and ground truth binary masks: $\text{TP} = \hat{y} \wedge y$, $\text{FP} = \hat{y} \wedge \neg y$, $\text{FN} = \neg \hat{y} \wedge y$. The resulting boolean masks are composited onto the original image at 50% opacity for spatial context.

## 5. Boundary Localization Analysis

Forensic applications place particular emphasis on boundary accuracy. A predicted mask that captures the interior of a tampered region but erodes or dilates the boundary by several pixels may correctly identify the presence of manipulation while misrepresenting its spatial extent. In legal or investigative contexts, such imprecision can be problematic.

Boundary analysis compares the contours of predicted and ground truth masks directly. Edge maps are extracted from both masks using a Canny edge detector or morphological gradient operation, and the two edge sets are rendered in contrasting colors — for example, ground truth contours in green and predicted contours in red — overlaid on the original image. Regions where the two edge sets coincide indicate accurate boundary localization. Systematic offsets reveal consistent dilation or erosion behavior, while ragged predicted edges suggest that the model lacks confidence at manipulation boundaries.

This analysis is particularly relevant for splicing forgeries, where the boundary between the pasted region and the host image often carries the strongest forensic artifacts (edge discontinuities, illumination shifts, noise pattern mismatches). A model that localizes these boundaries precisely is leveraging genuine manipulation signals rather than relying on coarse spatial heuristics.

## 6. Robustness Testing Visualization

Tampered images encountered outside controlled benchmarks are frequently subjected to post-processing operations — social media platforms apply JPEG recompression, resizing, and sharpening as part of their upload pipelines. A forensically useful model must maintain detection capability under such transformations.

Robustness testing applies controlled degradations to test images and visualizes the resulting predictions alongside clean-image predictions. The standard degradation suite includes:

- **JPEG compression** at quality factors 50 and 70, which destroys high-frequency forensic artifacts.
- **Gaussian noise** at varying intensities, which masks subtle pixel-level inconsistencies.
- **Gaussian blurring**, which smooths edge-based tampering evidence.
- **Downscale-upscale cycles** at 0.5× and 0.75× factors, which introduce resampling artifacts while destroying original resolution detail.

For each condition, the four-panel visualization (original, ground truth, prediction, overlay) is regenerated and compared with the clean baseline. Side-by-side presentation makes performance degradation immediately visible: masks that shrink, fragment, or vanish entirely under compression reveal the model's dependence on artifacts that do not survive recompression. Consistent performance across degradation levels, conversely, provides evidence that the model has learned robust manipulation features.

## 7. Failure Case Analysis

Systematic analysis of failure cases is essential for guiding model improvements. In the context of image tampering detection, failure modes cluster around several recurring scenarios:

- **Very small tampered regions** (below 2% of image area) produce minimal foreground signal relative to the overwhelming background. Models trained with unweighted losses tend to suppress predictions in these regions entirely.
- **Low-texture areas** such as sky, walls, or uniform backgrounds provide few discriminative features for localization. The model may detect that tampering is present at the image level but cannot resolve its spatial extent.
- **Heavy JPEG compression** eliminates the high-frequency artifacts that many detection methods rely upon, particularly double-compression traces and interpolation residuals.
- **Copy-move manipulation** duplicates regions from within the same image, preserving lighting, noise characteristics, and compression history. This makes copy-move inherently harder to detect than splicing, where the pasted region originates from a different source.
- **Complex illumination variations** can cause authentic shadow boundaries or lighting gradients to be misinterpreted as tampering artifacts, generating false positives.

Each failure case is documented with the full visualization suite — prediction grid, probability heatmap, and error map — accompanied by analysis of the likely cause. This documentation creates a structured record that directly informs subsequent experimentation: if copy-move failures dominate, the training pipeline may require dedicated copy-move augmentation or specialized feature extraction modules.

## 8. Best, Typical, and Worst Case Examples

To provide a calibrated understanding of model performance, three representative prediction categories are visualized:

**Best case** examples are selected as tampered images with the highest Pixel-F1 scores. These typically involve large, clearly delineated splicing regions with strong boundary contrast. The predicted mask aligns closely with the ground truth, exhibiting precise contours and minimal false positive spillover. These cases confirm that the model has learned meaningful tampering features and can produce forensically useful output.

**Typical case** examples are drawn from the median F1 range. These predictions capture the general location of the tampered region but exhibit moderate boundary errors — slight dilation, minor fragmentation, or partial misses at region extremities. Typical cases represent the expected operational performance of the model and provide the most honest assessment of its practical utility.

**Failure case** examples correspond to the lowest F1 scores, including complete misses (F1 = 0.0). These are analyzed in detail through error maps and probability heatmaps to determine whether the model produced no signal at all (feature extraction failure) or weak signal below the classification threshold (thresholding sensitivity). Failure cases involving copy-move forgeries where the texture of the source and target match closely, or involving very small tampered regions below the model's effective resolution, are of particular diagnostic value.

Presenting all three categories together prevents both overconfidence from cherry-picked successes and excessive pessimism from worst-case focus.

---

## 9. Summary

Visualization is not an auxiliary component of the evaluation pipeline — it is a primary analytical tool. Quantitative metrics such as IoU, F1, precision, and recall compress the spatial complexity of segmentation predictions into scalar values, which, while useful for comparison and benchmarking, cannot capture the forensically relevant details of prediction quality.

The visualization methodology described in this document — spanning detection grids, probability heatmaps, error maps, boundary analysis, robustness visualizations, and structured failure case studies — provides a comprehensive framework for assessing whether a tamper detection model produces outputs that are spatially coherent, boundary-accurate, and robust to real-world degradations. For a U-Net architecture with a ResNet34 encoder operating on datasets such as CASIA, this qualitative evaluation is essential for validating that the model is learning manipulation-specific features rather than exploiting dataset-level shortcuts or compression artifacts.

Qualitative and quantitative evaluation are complementary. Neither alone is sufficient; together, they provide the evidence necessary to determine whether a deep learning based tamper detection and localization system meets the reliability standards required for forensic applications.
